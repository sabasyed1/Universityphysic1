Velocity and acceleration - Measuring acceleration due to gravity¶

Objective:¶

The goal of this experiment is to study the motion of an object sliding down an incline, and how the effect of changing height (and angle) of an incline influences the velocity and acceleration of the object rolling down. The velocity and acceleration of a cart will be calculated, graphed, and analyzed to find the value of g.¶

Introduction:¶

All objects experience acceleration due to gravity during free fall motion (acceleration due gravity is denoted as $g$). We know this acceleration is about $9.8~m/s^2$ at sea level. Rather than dropping objects off buildings or jumping out of airplanes, we will time an object (a cart) as it rolls freely down an inclined plane (ramp). By timing the fall (or roll down) of the cart and measuring the distance rolled, we can calculate the displacement from the initial position, the average velocity, and the final velocity of the cart and determine its acceleration due to gravity down an incline at different heights. Then we can graph the data, and use the slope of the line to calculate $g$.¶

2. Wooden blocks or 4 textbooks¶

Theory:¶

Objects under acceleration experiences an increase in their velocity with time. If the acceleration is a constant $a$, and if the object starts from a position of rest i.e., $v_{\rm initial} = 0$, then the final velocity after time $t$ is given by $v_{\rm final} = at$. Thus It is possible to calculate the acceleration experienced by an object calculating. Since the average velocity is just the distance $s$, divided by time $t$, and since $s = \frac{1}{2}at^2$, we can write $v_{\rm avg} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{at^2}{t} = \frac{1}{2}at = v_{\rm final}/2$. Thus we can calculate the acceleration of the systems using the average velocity as $a = 2v_{\rm avg}/t$.¶

We will use this concept of average velocity to calculate the acceleration of a cart rolling down the inclined plank. The expected acceleration of the cart is given by $a = g\sin\theta$. If we calculate the angle $\theta$ and the acceleration using the average velocity above, we can then calculate the acceleration due to gravity:¶

$g = 2\frac{v_{\rm avg}}{t\sin\theta}$¶

Procedure:¶

1. On a clear table top set up an inclined plane as in the diagram above using the $1.2 m$ long track or a wooden board. The track already has a metric scale built in. If you are using a wooden board tap a carpenter ruler with the zero point at the top of the board. Raise the $0$m end at a height of $0.05$ to $0.1 m$ (or $5$ to $10$cm around $3$ inches). (use block of wood, or 1 Physics textbook for this height). lay your phone using PhysicsToolbox to find the incline angle. you can also use the right triangle formula below to find the angle of the incline¶

$\sin\theta = \frac{{\rm height~of~the~upper~end}}{{\rm length~of~the~board}}$¶

2. The zero of the scale should be at the upper end/ Hold the cart at the upper end of the board as far away from the lower end possible. Note the reading on the scale where front of the cart is placed. This is your starting value of position $x_0$.¶

3. Now release the cart carefully, without giving any forward, backward or sidways push so that the initial velocity is zero. Right at the moment of releasing the cart start your stopwatch. If you are using a phone then it is very important that you ask someone else to measure time.¶

4. Let the stopwatch run as long as the cart is sliding. The moment the cart hits the wall at the other end, stop the stopwatch.¶

5. Note down the time, and then repeat it for four more times.¶

6. Calculate the average time taken for this point of $x_0$.¶

7. Now change the position of the $x_0$ to a new point $\sim 10$ cm away from the first point.¶

8. Repeat steps 3, 4, 5, and 6.¶

9. Continue repeating this for a total of $10$ different value of $x_0$.¶

10. Then change angle of the incline to a higher value by raising the upper end by using another block/book, and repeat the full experiment for this new angle.¶

11. Repeat this again for a third angle.¶

Root mean square error¶

When the true value is known one can also calculate the root-mean-square error. This is done by first computing the difference of the value from the true value and squaring it. Then we sum all these squares and take the mean. And finally we take the square root of the result.¶

$\sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum(g - 9.8)^2}$¶

Finally, we will calculate the statistical uncertainty in our measurement, that doesn't require us to know the true value of $g$. This is done by calculating the standard deviation. The standard deviation of the value of $g$ is given by:¶

$\sigma (g)= \sqrt{\bar{g^2} - (\bar{g})^2}$¶

Where, $\bar{g}$ is the average value of $g$ and $\bar{g^2}$ is the average value of $g^2$.¶
